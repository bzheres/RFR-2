Introduction to Anatomy

Date: July 16, 2024

Introduction

Before starting to learn anatomy, it is important to understand the different terminology that is used in this field. This article will cover the basics to build a solid foundation:

  • The anatomical position.
  • The anatomical planes.
  • Terminology to describe structures relative to one another.
  • Terminology to describe movement.

    • Anatomical Position

    The anatomical position is the standard body orientation that anatomical terms are then based off. The anatomical position is when the body is positioned:

  • Upright
  • Feet spread, about shoulder-width apart
  • Arms hanging comfortably by the side
  • Palms facing forward

    Anatomical Planes

    Next, anatomical planes are hypothetical planes dividing the body into defferent sections. There are three anatomical planes to know:

  • The coronal plane.
  • The sagittal plane.
  • The transverse plane.

    • The coronal plane, which is also called the frontal plane, passes through the body vertically. It separates the body into anterior and posteerior sections (terms that will be covered in the next section - Relative Terminology). This can easily be remembered by memorising that the frontal plane splits the body into front (anterior) and back (posterior) sections.

    The sagittal plane also runs vertically. However, this runs at a right angle to the coronal (frontal) plane to divide the body into equal right and left sections. If the sagittal plane is imagined run through the body vertically slightly off center, it no longer splits the body into equal sections and is called the parasagittal plane.

    The third plane, the transverse plane, also called the axial plane, passes through the body horizontally, parallel to the ground. This planedivides the body into superior and inferior sections (terms that will be covered in the next section - Relative Terminology).

    Relative Terminology

    You may have heard of terms like "anterior" and posterior" before, such as above. These are terms that can be used to describe the position of one body structure relative to another. The use of these terms allow for much more precision when communicating with others. The terms used are based off the anatomical position.

    Superior

    Superior: Directed toward the head or upper part of a strucutre; positioned above.

    E.g. The knee is superior to the foot.

    Inferior

    Inferior: Directed toward the feet or lower part of a structure; positioned below.

    E.g. The foot is inferior to the knee.

    Anterior

    Anterior: Directed toward the front of the body; positioned in front.

    E.g. The bellybutton is anterior to the spine.

    Posterior

    Posterior: Directed toward the back of the body; positioned behind.

    E.g. The spine is posterior to the bellybutton.

    Medial

    Medial: Directed toward the midline of the body; positioned on the inner side.

    E.g. The spine is medial to the arms.

    Lateral

    Lateral: Directed away from the midline of the body; positioned on the outer side.

    E.g. The arms are lateral to the spine.

    Intermediate

    Intermediate: Positioned between a more medial and a more lateral structure.

    E.g. The sternum (breastbone) is intermediate between the clavicles (collar bones).

    Proximal

    Proximal: Closer to the origin of the body part or the origin of the attachment of a limb.

    E.g. The knee is proximal to the ankle.

    Distal

    Distal: Farther from the origin of a body part or point of attachment of a limb to the body trunk.

    E.g. The ankle is distal to the knee.

    Superficial/External

    Superficial/External: Dorected towards, or at the body surface; positioned more external.

    E.g. The skin is superfical to the bones of the skeleton.

    Deep/Internal

    Deep/Internal: Directed inwards from the body surface; positioned more internal.

    E.g. The bones of the skeleton are deep to the skin.

    Describing Movements

    Now lets utilise what we have covered so far and learn some terms that are used to describe different movements.

    Flexion and Extension

    Flexion describes reducing the angle between two body parts while extension describes increasing the angle. These two movements generally occur in the sagittal plane involving anterior or posterior movements of the neck, body trunk, or limbs.

    For example, if you were to "flex" your biceps, it requires bringing your forearm towards the upper arm. This reduces the angle at the elbow and this is flexion. Straightening your arm back to a neutral position is extension.

    Adduction and Abduction

    Adduction describes bringing one body apart towards the midline while abduction describes moving it away from the midline.

    For example, starting from the anatomical position, the movement of bringing your arms out to the side at a 90 degree angle is abduction. This can easily be remembered as you are "abducting" your arms from the midline, so you are stealing your arms away from the midline. The opposite, bringing your arms from 90 degrees back towards the midline is adduction. This can also easily be remembered as you are "adding" your arms back to the side of your body.

    Lateral and Medial Rotation

    Lateral rotation, also called external rotation, describes the momevent that rotates a limbs anterior surface laterally (i.e. away from the midline).

    The opposite, medial rotation, which is also called internal rotation, therefore is when the anterior surface of a limb is moved medially.

    For example, if you were to rotate a striaght leg so that the toes were facing inwards (medially), this would be medial rotation. If you were to do the opposite and rotate the leg so the toes are now facing away from your body, this would be lateral rotation.

    Pronation and Supination

    While pronation and supination can be easily confused with lateral and medial rotation, they are distinct movements that you should understand.

    Supination describes the position of the forearm when it is in the anatomical position with the palms facing forwards.

    Pronation describes moving the forearm from the anatomical position so the palm now faces backwards.

    These two terms can also be applied to the whole body. For example, a supine position is when a person lies flat on their back facing the ceiling. Meanwhile, lying flat, face down, is the prone position. This should be easy to remember for anyone who has ever had to do the "prone hold" exercise, as your body is in the prone position during this exercise.

    Dorsiflexion and Plantar Flexion

    These two terms are specific to flexion and extension of the ankle. So rather than describing ankle movements as flexion or extension, bringing your toes up towards your shin, like walking on your heels, is called dorsiflexion. Meanwhile, pointing your toes down, walking on tippy toes, is called planter flexion.

    Eversion and Inversion

    Like dorsiflexion and plantar flexion, eversion and inversion are also used to describe ankle movements.

    The term eversion describes moving the inferior surface of your foot (sole) away from the median plane (laterally). The opposite movement, inversion describes rotating the foot so the inferior surface of your foot is now facing medially.

    There is a greater range of motion when inverting the foot compared to when everting the foot, and as you learn the anatomy of the ankle you will understand why. You can give these movements a try and see for yourself!

    Opposition and Reposition

    These are movements of the hand. Opposition is the movement that brings the thumb together with the tip of the other fingers. Meanwhile, reposition is the movement that moves them away from each other. Thumb opposition is actually the combination of two other movements discussed above: flexion and abduction.

    Protraction and Retraction

    These movements can be described using the scapule (shoulder blade) as an example.

    Protraction (or protrusion) describes the anterior and lateral movement of the scapula on the posterior thoracic wall. This movement alows the shoulder to move anteriorly, like when reaching for something in front of you.

    The opposite movement, retraction (or retrusion) causes the scapula to move posteriorly and medially.

    These terms can also be applied to other structures like the mandible (the bone that forms the lower jaw) for example.

    Elevation and Depression

    These movements describe moving in the superior or inferior direction.

    For example, shrugging your shoulders uses these movements. Shrugging your shoulders up is elevation while drooping your shoulders is depression.

    Circumduction

    Circumduction refers to movement that occurs in a circular manner, where one end stays still while the distal end moves in a circular motion.

    This movement uses a sequention combination of other movements already discussed: flexion, adduction, extension, abduction.

    Conclusion

    You should now have a good understanding of what the anatomical position is, what the three anatomical planes are, and what different terms are used to describe structures position and movements.

    As you begin to learn anatomy, you will realise knowing these basics is essential for being able to picture how different structures relate to each other. You can always refer back to this article if you need some revision, otherwise, good luck as you start your anatomical studies!
