Abdominal Aorta

Date: August 8, 2024

Introduction

The abdominal aorta is the continuation of the thoracic aorta as it passes through the aortic hiatus. Within the abdomen, the abdominal aorta runs anterior to the lumbar vertebral bodies and to the left of the inferior vena cava. It terminates at the bifurcation of the left and right common iliac arteries, which continue into the pelvis and lower limbs to supply blood to these areas. Along its path through the abdomen, the abdominal aorta gives off several paired and unpaired branches.1,2

Aortic Hiatus

The aortic hiatus is an opening in the diaphragm situated between the left and right crura anteriorly and the vertebral column posteriorly (Figure 1).3 It is positioned slightly to the left of the vertebral column.


The thoracic aorta passes through the aortic hiatus at the level of the T12 vertebral body, where it becomes the abdominal aorta. Technically, the aorta does not pass directly through the diaphragm but rather behind it.2,4,5


In some individuals, the aortic hiatus may be surrounded by a fibrous ring. This occurs when some diaphragmatic fibers extend medially from the crura, posterior to the aorta, and anterior to the vertebral column.5


In addition to the aorta, the azygos vein and the thoracic duct also pass through the aortic hiatus.5

Branches

Unpaired Anterior Branches

  • Coeliac Artery: Supplies the foregut.
  • Superior Mesenteric Artery: Supplies the midgut.
  • Inferior Mesenteric Artery: Supplies the hindgut.
  • Note: These are typically best viewed in the sagittal plane on imaging.

    • Paired Lateral Branches

  • Renal Arteries: Supply the kidneys.
  • Adrenal (Suprarenal) Arteries: Supply the adrenal glands.
  • Gonadal (Testicular/Ovarian) Arteries: Supply the testes in males and ovaries in females.
  • Note: These are typically best viewed in the coronal plane on imaging.

    • Other Branches

  • Paired Inferior Phrenic Arteries: Supply the diaphragm.
  • Variable number of Paired Lumbar Arteries: Usually 4 paired arteries; supply the first four lumbar vertebrae, muscles of the back, and intervertebral discs.
  • Unpaired Median Sacral Artery: Supplies the rectum.

    • Collateral Supply

  • Artery of Adamkiewicz: Provides collateral supply to the lower spinal cord and arises either from a lumbar artery or a posterior intercostal artery near near the thoracolumbar junction.2

    Termination

    The abdominal aorta terminates at the bifurcation of the left and right common iliac arteries at the level of the L4 vertebral body.2,4

    Clinical Application

    Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) is a dilatation of the aorta by 50% or more of its anteroposterior diameter.2


    The majority of AAA are infrarenal (see Figure 7) and are typically identified before they are large enough to meet the threshold for intervention which is indicated when the diameter is between 5-5.5cm depending on patient factors.2,11

    Variations

    Accessory renal arteries branching off the abdominal aorta is a common finding.2


    Variants for the branches off the abdominal aorta are discussed on their own page.
